1. Introductions 
City Council President Ralph Pell welcomed those in attendance and noted it had been a significant amount 
of time since the City and County had convened for a monthly update meeting. Mr. Pell stated that the 
meeting was intended as an opportunity to re-establish communication and collaboration between the two 
governing bodies. 
Introductions were made by those present.

Mr. Pell and Mr. Kubik provided background on the historical purpose of the City-County Monthly Update 
meetings. It was explained that these meetings traditionally served as an opportunity for the two policy 
bodies to discuss matters of mutual interest, provide updates on significant projects and issues, and identify 
opportunities for collaboration on concerns affecting both City and County residents. 
Mr. Kubik explained that prior meetings typically included one or more specific discussion items on the 
agenda, such as homelessness, economic development, emergency management, or other major community 
issues. He noted that because this was the first meeting in some time, no specific substantive agenda items 
had been prepared, but future meetings could include focused topics for discussion. 
Mr. Pell spoke regarding prior instances in which the City and County may have benefited from stronger 
collaboration. One example discussed was a previous state funding opportunity related to homelessness 
initiatives. Mr. Pell stated the City of Grants Pass had declared a homeless state of emergency, which was 
required in order to access certain state funds, but the Board of Commissioners at that time had declined to 
do the same. As a result, the community was unable to pursue that funding path jointly. 
Discussion followed regarding the differing philosophies and perspectives of prior governing bodies and how 
those differences had affected the ability of the City and County to work together. Mr. Kubik noted the City 
had attempted to move the matter forward, but the State required County participation as well. It was 
acknowledged that the inability to collaborate at that time resulted in a missed funding opportunity.

Commissioner Smith stated his understanding that prior concern from the Board had been that declaring an 
emergency could potentially provide the State with greater authority over local affairs. Mr. Pell recalled 
hearing that rationale previously as well. Members generally agreed that, whether or not such concerns were 
legally well-founded, the situation illustrated the importance of maintaining open communication and 
cooperation going forward. 
The group also discussed that City residents are also residents and constituents of Josephine County and that 
issues affecting the City are also matters of concern to the County. It was noted that County collaboration 
should include the interests of residents within all incorporated areas of the County, including both Grants 
Pass and Cave Junction. 
Mr. Pell raised a prior issue involving the Dollar Mountain Trail project. He stated that, at one point, the City 
had sought to purchase a County-owned parcel that was located in an area that would have benefited the trail 
project. He described the parcel as steep, landlocked terrain with limited apparent value for other purposes, 
but potentially useful for recreational trail connectivity. 
Mr. Pell stated that he had attempted to gather additional information regarding whether the parcel remained 
valuable to the City for that purpose. Discussion followed regarding the possibility of determining whether 
the property is still of interest to the City and whether the County would have interest in transferring or 
otherwise making the parcel available for recreational use. 
Commissioner Martin agreed to reach out to County Property Manager Helene regarding additional 
information on the parcel. It was the consensus of the group that the matter could be placed on a future 
monthly update agenda for more focused discussion after staff gathered the relevant information. 
Mr. Pell next discussed vacancies on the joint bikeways and walkways committee, describing it as a joint 
City-County committee with ten members, five appointed from the City side and five from the County side. 
He stated there had been two County-side vacancies for an extended period and that applicants had 
previously applied, been interviewed by the committee, and been recommended for appointment, but the 
appointments had never been finalized by the Board of Commissioners. 
Mr. Pell explained that the lack of full membership had made it difficult for the committee to consistently 
achieve a quorum, particularly during summer months when attendance could be reduced. He stated that if 
the previously identified applicants were still interested, the issue might be easily resolved by contacting 
them and proceeding with appointments. 
Discussion followed regarding the committee structure and the role of staff liaison support. Mr. Pell stated 
that the committee’s work is often more City-centered, although County road and infrastructure issues do 
arise and make County participation appropriate. He suggested that a strong staff liaison can often provide 
sufficient County involvement without requiring regular commissioner attendance. 
Commissioners indicated interest in reviewing the status of the prior applicants and determining whether 
appointments could be made to fill the vacant positions. 
The group discussed economic development as a major area of future City-County collaboration. Reference 
was made to previous collaborative economic development efforts and to the evolution of those efforts over 
time. Commissioner Smith noted that current organizational structures and economic development resources 
are in transition, and that this may not be the ideal time to recreate prior committee structures, but 
emphasized that supporting business growth and economic vitality remains a shared priority.

Discussion followed regarding the importance of making investment in both the City and County easier 
rather than more difficult. Members noted that most economic development activity is likely to occur in and 
around the urban growth areas associated with Grants Pass because of state land use laws and development 
patterns. It was suggested that economic development funding and tools available to the City and County 
may be able to complement one another when opportunities arise. 
Mr. Kubik explained that the City primarily utilizes transient lodging tax funds for tourism and related 
economic development work and that such funds have statutory limitations on their use. The group also 
referenced prior City financial support for airport improvements as an example of a successful overlap 
between City and County interests for economic development purposes. 
Potential future agenda topics identified under this broader category included economic development 
strategy, job creation facilitation, and airport-related development opportunities. 
Mr. Kubik suggested that future agenda items could include emergency management, Firewise 
programming, and DMIC. He indicated these were subjects likely to be of interest to both policy bodies. 
At the request of the Commissioners, Mr. Kubik provided additional explanation regarding Firewise. He 
stated that the City and County had previously partnered on Firewise efforts, with two Firewise coordinators 
serving the community, one managed by the County and one through the City fire department. He stated the 
program had been successful, but at some point the County’s funding support was withdrawn, reducing the 
capacity of the overall program. 
Mr. Kubik emphasized the importance of Firewise work in Josephine County due to the County’s wildfire 
risk, particularly in the wildland-urban interface. He described Firewise as a program that helps educate 
communities, work with property owners and neighborhoods, and improve resiliency against fire through 
prevention and mitigation efforts. 
Discussion followed regarding the insurance-related benefits of Firewise participation. It was noted that 
Firewise community designation and property assessments can help residents address increased insurance 
costs or preserve coverage in the wake of wildfire risk mapping and rural insurance changes. Reference was 
made to Michael McLaughlin’s work in assisting property owners and communities with Firewise-related 
assessments and organization. 
Commissioner Smith noted that Michael McLaughlin had recently approached the County regarding the 
possibility of adding additional staffing support for the program, though there remains uncertainty regarding 
federal funding and grant timing. Members agreed Firewise should remain on the table for future discussion 
and likely warrants priority consideration as fire season approaches. 
The group briefly referenced DMIC as another subject that could benefit from future joint discussion, 
including the County’s vision and the potential role of the City in broader planning conversations. No 
substantive discussion on DMIC occurred beyond identifying it as a future agenda item. 
The group discussed infrastructure issues involving the North Valley area, the airport, and the need for 
coordination between the City and County on future development. Commissioner Smith asked about the 
City’s water service extending to the North Valley area outside City limits.

Mr. Kubik explained that the City had extended water service in part due to historic contamination concerns 
associated with the Merlin landfill and in connection with the North Valley Industrial Park. He stated this 
water extension also benefits the airport and animal shelter area. 
Discussion followed regarding water line extension to the airport and the importance of continued 
collaboration due to the airport’s location in the County but its close functional relationship to the City. 
The primary obstacle identified for further development in that area was wastewater and sewer infrastructure. 
It was explained that the Three Rivers School District currently operates the treatment facility serving the 
North Valley Industrial Park and associated school properties, but desires to exit that role. Discussion 
addressed the need for upgrades so treated water could reach a standard suitable for spray irrigation, thereby 
improving the system’s discharge options and creating greater usable capacity. 
Mr. Kubik explained that the issue is not primarily one of inflow volume, but rather of outflow and treatment 
capability. Discussion also addressed the technical challenges of wastewater system operation, including the 
limited number of qualified and certified operators available to oversee such systems. The difference 
between City-operated utility systems and the school district’s facility management arrangement was also 
discussed. 
Commissioner Smith noted the issue is significant and will need to be addressed in future discussions, 
particularly if the industrial park area is to support further economic development. It was also noted that 
County Legal Counsel Wally Hicks had been involved in the matter and that a future update could be 
provided. 
Commissioner Smith asked whether the City of Grants Pass has a similar collaborative relationship with the 
City of Cave Junction. Mr. Kubik stated that while there is not a formal recurring policy-board meeting 
structure between Grants Pass and Cave Junction, there has been some overlap through service-related 
committees and commissions, and City officials occasionally communicate informally with officials from 
other jurisdictions. 
Mr. Pell stated that, from a council perspective, he had not typically viewed Cave Junction as a routine 
source of comparative policy guidance, due in part to the difference in city size and scale of operations. 
However, it was acknowledged that Cave Junction’s economic well-being remains important to the County 
as a whole and has a connection to Grants Pass as the County seat and regional shopping and service center. 
Commissioners expressed that, from the County’s perspective, it is important to see both incorporated cities 
within Josephine County succeed, and that the County may be well-positioned to help facilitate coordination 
where beneficial. 
Commissioner Smith raised the question of how the City and County could work together to rebuild trust 
among the public and demonstrate that both policy bodies are working on behalf of their constituents. 
Mr. Kubik responded that re-establishing regular monthly meetings is a strong first step and that 
collaboration will gain credibility as it begins to produce practical results. He stated that, over time, mutual 
projects and common interests would likely emerge from continued discussion. 
Mr. Pell agreed and stated that even in circumstances where the City and County are unable to collaborate 
directly on a particular issue, regular communication and a rational public explanation of limitations or
